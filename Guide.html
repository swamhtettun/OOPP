Introduction: What Is Smart Home:

Smart home technology, sometimes referred as home automation, provides homeowners security, comfort, convenience, and energy efficiency by allowing them to control smart devices through the use of a smart home app in their phones or other devices. Smart home systems and devices often operate together as a part of the internet of things(IoT), sharing consumer usage data among themselves, automating actions based on the homeowners' preferences.


Smart home was realised in 1975, when X10, a communication protocol for smart home was introduced. It sends radio frequency (RF) bursts of digital information onto a home's existing electric wiring to programmable outlets or switches, conveying commands to corresponding devices, controlling how and when devices operate. For example, a transmitter can send a signal along the house's electric wiring, telling a device to turn on at a specific time.


However, since electrical wiring isn't designed to be free from radio-band "noise", X10 was not always fully reliable. Signals would be lost, and while smart homes can take commands, they cannot send data back to a central network, as X10 was initially a one-way technology. Luckily, two-way X10 devices became available at a later date, with a much higher cost.

Examples of smart home technologies:

With technology as advanced as today, nearly every aspect of life have been introduced with technology:

  • Smart Television: Connects to the internet to access content through application, such as on-demand video and music; some also include voice or gesture recognition.
  • Smart Lighting Systems: In addition to being able to be controlled remotely and customized, it can detect when occupants are in the room and adjust lighting as needed.; the lightbulbs also regulate themselves based on daylight availability.
  • Kitchen/Laundry Appliances: They include smart coffee makers that can brew you a fresh cup as soon as your alarm goes off; smart refrigerators that keep track of expiration dates, make shopping lists or even create recipes based on ingredients currently on hand; slower cookers and toasters; and, in the laundry room, washing machines and dryers.
